## PART 3: THREE TYPES OF UAS (Aerodynamic Classification)

### 1. Fixed Wing
- **Source of lift:** Fixed wing (airfoil shape)
- Requires relative airflow over the wing to generate lift
- **Advantages:**
  - Larger endurance (longer flight time)
  - Greater range
  - Easier control
  - Technology is highly scalable (100 g to 3+ tons)
  - Lower power consumption for lift generation
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Cannot hover
  - Cannot take off/land vertically — needs runway, catapult, or launcher
  - Susceptible to stall if it slows too much
- **Example:** General Atomics MQ-1 Predator, MQ-9 Reaper (MALE drones); DRDO Rustom, Nishant (India)

### 2. Rotary Wing
- **Source of lift:** Spinning rotor (blade)
- Rotor always moves relative to air → lift always available
- **Advantages:**
  - Can hover
  - High maneuverability at low speeds
  - Vertical takeoff and landing (VTOL)
  - Good for confined/indoor spaces
  - Technology scalable
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Inherently unstable in hover — requires autopilot stabilization
  - Lower endurance
  - Noisier
- **Example:** Northrop Grumman MQ-8 Fire Scout (autonomous helicopter UAV)

### 3. Flapping Wing
- **Source of lift:** Unsteady lift generated by flapping motion
- **Advantages:**
  - Can fly at very low speeds (some can hover like hummingbirds)
  - Excellent camouflage (looks like a real bird)
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Mechanical complexity
  - Scalability is a major challenge (nature itself limits bird size)
  - Low actuator power — vulnerable to wind (>5 m/s can cause backward flight)
  - Still a research area — no commercial product yet
- **Example:** AeroVironment Nano Hummingbird ($3 million for 2 prototypes, ~19 g); Festo SmartBird

## PART 10: PHYSICS OF FLUID FLOW (Lecture 3)

### Key Physical Quantities

**Pressure (p)**
- Force per unit area: p = lim (dF/dA) as dA → 0
- Unit: N/m²  (Pascals)

**Density (ρ)**
- Mass per unit volume
- Unit: kg/m³
- Air at room temperature: **ρ = 1.225 kg/m³**

**Temperature (T)**
- Measure of average kinetic energy of gas particles
- KE = 1.5 kT, where k = Boltzmann constant = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K

**Flow Velocity (V)**
- Velocity of an infinitesimal fluid element at a fixed point in the flow

**Streamlines**
- Path traced by a moving fluid element
- In steady flow, streamlines = actual particle paths

### Sources of Aerodynamic Forces
All aerodynamic forces arise from only TWO sources:
1. **Pressure distribution** — acts normal (perpendicular) to the surface
   - Upper surface: low pressure (suction)
   - Lower surface: high pressure
2. **Shear stress (τ_w)** — acts tangential (parallel) to the surface (viscous friction)

## PART 11: HOW LIFT IS GENERATED

### Concept of Lift (Bernoulli + Newton)
When air flows over an airfoil:
- Upper surface: air speeds up → pressure **decreases** (low pressure region / suction)
- Lower surface: air slows down → pressure **increases** (high pressure region)
- Net upward pressure force = **LIFT**

The wing pushes air downward → by Newton's 3rd Law, air pushes wing upward.

### Key Airfoil Terminology
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Chord line | Straight line from leading edge to trailing edge |
| Angle of attack (α) | Angle between chord line and oncoming airflow |
| Leading edge (LE) | Front tip of the airfoil |
| Trailing edge (TE) | Rear tip of the airfoil |
| Upper surface | Cambered (curved) side — low pressure |
| Lower surface | Flatter side — high pressure |
| Center of pressure | Point where the resultant pressure force acts |

### Effect of Angle of Attack (AoA)
| AoA | Behavior |
|-----|---------|
| 0° | Lift generated (mainly from camber), center of pressure at mid-chord |
| 6° | Negative pressure pattern extends, more lift, center of pressure moves forward |
| 12° | Maximum lift zone, center of pressure moves further forward |
| 18° | **STALL** — flow separates from upper surface, lift collapses |

**Stall** = condition where increasing angle of attack actually decreases lift because the smooth airflow over the upper surface breaks down (separates). Fixed wing aircraft cannot fly below stall speed.

## PART 12: KEY DIMENSIONLESS NUMBERS

### Reynolds Number (Re)

$$Re = \frac{\rho V L}{\mu}$$

Where:
- ρ = fluid density (kg/m³)
- V = flow velocity (m/s)
- L = reference length (chord length for a wing, in m)
- μ = dynamic viscosity of fluid (kg/m·s)

**Physical meaning:** Ratio of **inertial forces** to **viscous forces**

| Scale | Typical Re |
|-------|-----------|
| Full-scale aircraft (Boeing 737) | ~10⁶ – 10⁷ |
| Small UAVs | ~10⁴ – 10⁵ |
| Micro aerial vehicles | ~10³ – 10⁴ |
| Insects | ~10² – 10³ |

**Why it matters for UAVs:**
- At low Re (small/slow UAVs), viscous effects dominate → aerodynamics behaves very differently
- Lift-to-drag ratio (L/D) degrades significantly at low Re
- This is why small drones have poor endurance — this is the fundamental answer to the DARPA MAV challenge

**Relationship:** As Re ↑ → L/D ↑ (up to a point), but drag also increases at transonic/supersonic speeds

### Mach Number (M)

$$M = \frac{V}{c}$$

Where:
- V = local flow velocity (m/s)
- c = local speed of sound (≈ 340 m/s at sea level, 20°C)

**Physical meaning:** Ratio of flow velocity to speed of sound

| Mach Range | Flow Regime |
|-----------|------------|
| M < 0.3 | Incompressible (most small UAVs) |
| 0.3 < M < 0.8 | Subsonic compressible |
| ~0.8 | Wave drag onset (drag rises sharply) |
| M > 1 | Supersonic |

**For most UAVs:** M << 0.3, so we treat airflow as incompressible. Compressibility effects are negligible.

## PART 13: ANATOMY OF A FIXED-WING AIRCRAFT

### Major Components
| Component | Function |
|-----------|---------|
| **Wing** | Generates lift |
| **Fuselage** | Main body; houses payload, fuel, electronics |
| **Propeller** | Generates thrust |
| **Powerplant** | Engine (IC or electric motor) |
| **Landing gear** | Supports aircraft on ground |
| **Empennage** | Tail assembly (horizontal + vertical stabilizer) |
| **Ailerons** | On wing trailing edge — control **roll** |
| **Elevator** | On horizontal tail — control **pitch** |
| **Rudder** | On vertical tail — control **yaw** |
| **Flaps** | Increase lift/drag at low speed (takeoff/landing) |
| **Slats** | Leading edge devices — extend low-speed capability |
| **Spoilers** | Reduce lift, increase drag (speed brakes / ground deceleration) |

### Three Axes of Control
1. **Roll** — rotation about the longitudinal axis (nose to tail) → controlled by **ailerons**
2. **Pitch** — rotation about the lateral axis (wing to wing) → controlled by **elevator**
3. **Yaw** — rotation about the vertical axis → controlled by **rudder**

### Subjective Questions with Answers

**Q1. Explain why a fixed-wing UAV cannot hover and why this limits its applications.**

**Answer:** A fixed-wing aircraft generates lift through the relative motion of air over the wing's asymmetric (cambered) profile. Lift is proportional to the square of the airspeed. At zero airspeed (hover), there is no relative airflow, hence no lift, and the aircraft falls — this is called stall. To maintain flight, the aircraft must continuously move forward above a minimum speed called the stall speed. This makes fixed-wing UAVs unsuitable for applications requiring stationary observation (e.g., inspecting a specific point on a structure), operations in confined spaces, or tasks requiring precision landing/takeoff without a runway. Rotary-wing platforms are preferred for such applications.

---

**Q2. Why does the DGCA classify drones by weight rather than size or speed?**

**Answer:** Weight is used as the classification criterion because it serves as a proxy for the kinetic energy (KE = ½mv²) of the drone. A drone of higher mass carries more kinetic energy at any given speed, and kinetic energy is the primary determinant of damage upon impact with a person or property. A 250 g nano drone crashing causes negligible damage, while a 150 kg large drone crashing could be fatal. Size alone is misleading (a small but dense object can be dangerous), and speed varies during operation. Weight provides a consistent baseline that directly correlates to potential damage, making it a sound engineering basis for regulatory tiers.

---

**Q3. Explain the significance of Reynolds Number for micro aerial vehicles (MAVs) compared to full-scale aircraft.**

**Answer:** The Reynolds Number (Re = ρVL/μ) characterizes the ratio of inertial to viscous forces in a fluid flow. For full-scale aircraft (e.g., Boeing 737), Re is on the order of 10⁶–10⁷, where inertial forces dominate and aerodynamic surfaces (wings, rotors) generate high lift-to-drag ratios (L/D). At these high Re values, the thin boundary layer is primarily turbulent but attached, and airfoils perform efficiently.

For MAVs (<100 g, chord lengths ~10–50 mm, speeds ~5–20 m/s), Re drops to 10³–10⁵. In this regime, viscous forces are significant, the boundary layer tends to remain laminar, and is highly susceptible to separation (forming laminar separation bubbles). This drastically degrades L/D. As a result, conventional airfoil designs used in full-scale aircraft perform poorly at MAV scales. This is the fundamental physical reason why achieving 60-minute endurance in a sub-100 g vehicle remains unachieved — the aerodynamic efficiency at low Re is simply much lower than at high Re.

**Q4. Compare fixed-wing, rotary-wing, and hybrid UAVs for a long-range agricultural pesticide spraying mission in a large open farm.**

**Answer:**
- **Fixed wing:** High endurance and range make it suitable for covering large areas quickly. However, it cannot hover over a specific section for precise spraying, requires a launch mechanism (catapult or runway), and is limited in payload mass it can carry.
- **Rotary wing (multirotor/helicopter):** Can hover and precisely target specific areas, take off/land vertically with no infrastructure. However, endurance is limited (typically 15–30 min for electric systems), restricting coverage per sortie. An IC-engine helicopter (like Yamaha RMAX) can carry larger payloads (40–50 kg) with longer endurance.
- **Hybrid VTOL:** Combines VTOL capability for takeoff/landing with efficient cruise flight. Suitable for larger farms but transition complexity and higher cost are concerns.

**Best choice for large, open flat farm:** A rotary-wing RUAV (like Yamaha RMAX/Fazer) or a hybrid VTOL. For very large areas, a fixed-wing with a drop mechanism could be adapted. Currently, rotary-wing platforms dominate agricultural spraying applications globally due to their precision hovering ability.

**Q5. What is the Mach Number and why is it less relevant for most small UAVs?**

**Answer:** Mach Number M = V/c is the ratio of local flow velocity to the local speed of sound (c ≈ 340 m/s at sea level, 20°C). It characterizes compressibility effects — whether the density of air changes significantly as it flows around a body.

For M < 0.3, the air behaves as essentially incompressible (density changes < 5%), and standard incompressible aerodynamic equations (Bernoulli, thin airfoil theory) apply directly. For M > 0.8, wave drag onset creates a sharp increase in drag. Beyond M = 1, shock waves form.

Most small UAVs fly at speeds of 10–150 km/h (≈ 3–42 m/s), giving M < 0.12. Since M << 0.3, compressibility is negligible and the flow can be treated as incompressible. Therefore, Mach number is not a primary design concern for small UAV aerodynamics — Reynolds number is far more significant.

**Q6. Describe the two sources of all aerodynamic forces and how they contribute to lift and drag.**

**Answer:** All aerodynamic forces on a body arise from only two physical sources:

1. **Pressure distribution:** The local air pressure acts normal (perpendicular) to the surface at every point. On a lifting airfoil, the upper surface has a low-pressure region (suction) due to accelerated flow, while the lower surface has higher pressure. The net integral of these pressure forces in the vertical direction gives **lift**. The component in the drag direction contributes to **pressure drag** (also called form drag).

2. **Shear stress (τ_w):** Viscous friction between the flowing air and the surface acts tangentially. On the upper surface, this acts in the downstream direction, contributing to **skin friction drag**. There is a very small lift contribution from shear stress on cambered surfaces, but it is generally negligible compared to pressure-induced lift.

Total Lift = ∫ pressure component (normal, upward) over entire surface
Total Drag = ∫ pressure component (normal, downstream) + ∫ shear stress component (tangential, downstream)

**Q7. Why have hybrid/VTOL UAVs not fully replaced both fixed-wing and rotary-wing platforms?**

**Answer:** Hybrid VTOL platforms face several key technical challenges:

1. **Transition complexity:** The flight regime change from hover (helicopter mode) to forward flight (airplane mode) is aerodynamically unstable and dynamically complex. The aerodynamic characteristics of the vehicle change dramatically during transition, and the autopilot must manage this smoothly.

2. **Weight penalty:** Carrying both fixed-wing surfaces and hover rotors adds structural weight that reduces payload fraction and overall efficiency. The airframe cannot be fully optimized for either regime.

3. **Autopilot complexity:** The control laws for a hybrid platform must handle three different modes (hover, transition, cruise) with very different dynamic behaviors — far more complex than a purely fixed-wing or rotary-wing controller.

4. **Cost and reliability:** Additional mechanical complexity (tilt mechanisms, additional motors) increases manufacturing cost and potential failure points.

5. **Efficiency trade-off:** A hybrid is never as efficient as a dedicated platform in either mode. A pure helicopter hovers more efficiently; a pure fixed-wing cruises more efficiently.

For missions clearly favoring one mode (precision hover for inspection vs. long-range survey), dedicated platforms remain preferable. Hybrids are ideal when the mission requires both VTOL and long-range cruise — a niche that is growing but not universal.
